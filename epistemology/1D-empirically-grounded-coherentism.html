According to philosophybasics.com: Foundationalism is the theory in Epistemology that beliefs can be justified based on basic or foundational beliefs (beliefs that give justificatory support to other beliefs). These basic beliefs are said to be self-justifying or self-evident, and do not need to be justified by other beliefs, being an inherently different kind of belief than a non-foundational one. Thus, a belief is epistemically justified either if it is justified by a basic belief or beliefs, or if it is justified by a chain of beliefs that is supported by a basic belief, and on which all the others are ultimately based.

The three positions share the underlying assumption that beliefs are justified solely by other beliefs, in a chain of reasoning. This is where I think things start to fall apart. I argue that beliefs are not just justified by other beliefs, but by their relation to subjective experience. Beliefs have intentionality. And yes, that is a belief. That belief has intentionality, in and of itself. So, that said, I would be a kind of holistic coherentist, with the caveat that beliefs don’t just cohere together, but also have intentionality, meaning they are about subjective experience, which cannot be doubted.

Another reason why foundationalism is not the fundamental way how the human brain structures its knowledge about the world is that it is possible for humans to create new beliefs that are not soundly grounded within the set of basic beliefs, axioms, and assumptions.

  • The primitive people who immediately conjured that the photo from the camera had stored spirits inside of it from the camera did not establish this new belief as something that follows only from what they believe in. Their internal philosophy for understanding how the world works is extremely in accurate, and while they probably do assume that spirits exist, they did not use any logic for concluding that the camera had stored spirit into the photo, nor do they explain how the camera would store spirit into the photo.
  • If they cannot explain how what they believe and works, yet they choose to hold the beliefs anyway, then they have chosen to believe in something that does not follow from their foundation because they had to make a new assumption about how what they perceive works. they made this new assumption not because human knowledge works according to foundationalism but rather because human knowledge works according to empirically grounded coherentism.
