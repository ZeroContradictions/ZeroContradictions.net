1. Introduction

The best way to challenge our beliefs is to debate them with other people and put our ideas to the test. I enjoyed reading Futurist Fantasies by T. K. Van Allen, and I believe it made a strong case for why space colonization is highly unlikely. It’s a great book, but there’s also a lot that can be added to it, particularly regarding technologies and ideas that it had not considered. My intention in writing this webpage is to further determine what humanity may be capable of, with greater accuracy.

2. Megastructures And Steel

The Megastructures chapter mainly focuses on the strength and weight of steel. Most futurists are aware that steel would be very heavy and possibly not strong enough for building space travel infrastructure. This is a list of materials that humanity could hypothetically use for megastructures instead of steel:

  • Carbon Fiber / Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs)
  • Kevlar

As of 2025, there is no proof that either of these materials can be used to affordably produced huge spacecraft for human colonization. All self-sustaining spacecraft that can transport sizable numbers of humans would need multiple square kilometers of surface. Maybe this will change in the future, but I think it’s unlikely.

3. O’Neill Cylinder Questions And Thought Experiments

3.1. The Original O’Neill Cylinder

I don’t believe that O’Neill wasn’t trying to create plans that were as detailed as much as possible when he was imagining the cylinder. It seems that he only wanted to sketch out the main ideas to get some discussion and thinking going. I base this conclusion on how he said that his proposals to mine materials from the Moon instead of the Earth were only intended to be “proofs of existence”, how he didn’t specify everything about how to build the cylinders, etc.

The dimensions and material design of the O’Neill Cylinder featured in Futurist Fantasies were based on the original O’Neill Cylinder concept, as stated on Wikipedia. The original O’Neill Cylinder design also used conventional design materials. Most spacecraft use aluminum, iron, or titanium. Nowadays, most futurists imagine spacecraft that is intended for interplanetary colonization to be made out of materials that are extremely expensive to create. It also hasn’t been demonstrated whether or not it’s viable to build such large structures.

Additionally, the Futurist Fantasies O’Neill Cylinder had an imagined length that was reduced to 10 kilometers long, in order to make it physically possible to build the cylinder with steel. Van Allen calculated how much it would cost just to send one cylinder into Low Earth orbit:

  • The Square/Cube Law makes it very difficult to build advanced megastructures like space elevators, orbital rings, etc.
  • 12km is the maximum length that a steel cable can support its own mass at Earth surface gravity. If it is any longer, it will snap under its own weight.
  • O’Neill Cylinders will probably never be economically feasible to build. If we built an O’Neill Cylinder that’s 10km long and 6.4 km in diameter, with a 1m thick hull, then it would weigh 3 trillion kg (2 trillion kg of steel, with 1 trillion kg of material).
  • Putting 1 kg into LEO varies between $50,000 and $1,500. The lowest cost being the Falcon Heavy from SpaceX, but with only 3 completed launches, this is a somewhat optimistic estimate.
  • So, if we assume a cost of $1000/kg, then putting a 3 trillion kg cylinder into LEO would cost $3 quadrillion ($3,000,000,000,000,000), and that’s only for one cylinder.
  • For comparison, the world’s nominal GDP is less than 100 trillion dollars.

– Paraphrased/Summarized from Futurist Fantasies by T. K. Van Allen

If it really would cost an estimated $3 quadrillion to build and launch such a megastructure into LEO, then it’s clearly not feasible. The Futurist Fantasies O’Neill Cylinder also isn’t optimal for interplanetary travel. The cylinder is too long and wide, and the hull isn’t thick enough for radiation shielding or protection against potential micrometeorite impacts. If we consider more optimal dimensions, then the cylinder will become more likely to achieve its goals, as well as more economical to build.

3.2. Ideal O’Neill Cylinders

I would like to state some reasonable premises for these thought experiments:

  • I want O’Neill Cylinder designs that are optimal and likely to accomplish their intended goals.
  • I want O’Neill Cylinder designs that are likely to withstand interplanetary space travel.
  • I want to estimate how much it would cost to send such a Cylinder to Mars, or Martian orbit.
  • I am only interested in constructing spacecraft that are economically and physically possible to build.
  • I’m not interested in technologies that don’t exist, or hypothetical cost reductions that haven’t happened yet. We cannot assume that we can rely on such hypothetical and unseen progress. There are no guarantees that such progress will actually happen, unless it’s provably likely to occur. Unless it’s provably likely that such progress will actually happen, there are no guarantees that it will occur.
  • I want to know what could potentially go wrong with O’Neill Cylinders. Spacecraft have been accidentally destroyed in the past, so this is definitely worthy of consideration.

3.3. Why Smaller Cylinders Are Better

Before we ask Claude questions about O’Neill cylinders in the next section, we should establish why it makes sense to make the O’Neill cylinders as small as possible:

  • It will obviously take a lot of resources to build even a single O’Neill Cylinder.
  • Smaller structures are more feasible to build. Humans still haven’t managed to build buildings that are taller than a kilometer.
  • Smaller structures will require less energy to launch.
  • As mentioned before, the spacehook seems like one of the most promising space launching infrastructure ideas out there.
    • In order to make spacehooks more viable, a constant stream of spacecraft flying between Earth and Mars would be necessary, in order to balance out the gravitational pulls for each spacecraft.
    • Humanity will have more spacecraft to accomplish this, if the spacecraft are each built smaller.
  • Smaller structures will be easier to mass produce. Hypothetically, a galactic space colonization will be much easier if all spacecraft use similar sizes and interchangeable parts.
  • Smaller structures minimize the potential losses and costs of failure.
  • The event that a rather small O’Neill cylinder accidentally falls towards the Earth is already dangerous enough as it is. If the same event happened with a cylinder that was the same size as the original design proposal, the impact would be strong enough to cause a mass extinction event that would probably wipe out most of the life on Earth.

Hypothetically, if the space infrastructure, space travel, and colonization all works out, then these life-sustaining spacecraft could be reused to colonize other planets, even after they finish their first journey(s).

The only conceivable advantage that bigger spacecraft may have is that they could sustain more people and resources. This could make the initial colonization of a planetary body easier, if the spacecraft successfully manages to land on the planet with all the life on it still living. However, larger cylinders simply aren’t very realistic. Smaller structures are more practical by every other measure. We will thus choose the smallest cylinder from the design proposals mentioned in the next section.

3.7. How much would it cost to launch that O’Neill Cylinder from Earth to Mars, when accounting for the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation?

How much would it cost to launch that O’Neill Cylinder from Earth to Mars, when accounting for the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation? Assume that humanity is dependent on currently existing rocket-launch technology. Please provide an equation for all these calculations.

I’ll break down the launch cost calculation using the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation, providing a comprehensive analysis of the launch economics for our Mars Direct Mission O’Neill Cylinder.

Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation Fundamentals:

Δv = ve * ln(m0 / mf)

Where:

  • Δv = Change in velocity
  • ve = Exhaust velocity
  • m0 = Initial total mass
  • mf = Final mass after propellant consumption

Launch Parameters:

  • Total Cylinder Mass: 129,000 metric tons (129,000,000 kg)
  • Current Best Rocket Technology: SpaceX Starship
    • Exhaust Velocity (ve): Approximately 3,500 m/s
    • Propellant Mass Fraction: ~0.92

Detailed Calculation:

  1. Initial Mass Calculation: m0 = Cylinder Mass + Propellant Mass
    m0 = 129,000,000 kg + (129,000,000 kg * 0.92)
    m0 = 129,000,000 kg + 118,680,000 kg
    m0 = 247,680,000 kg
  2. Final Mass Calculation: mf = Cylinder Mass
    mf = 129,000,000 kg
  3. Velocity Change Calculation: Δv = 3,500 m/s * ln(247,680,000 / 129,000,000)
    Δv = 3,500 m/s * ln(1.92)
    Δv = 3,500 m/s * 0.653
    Δv ≈ 2,285 m/s

Energy and Propellant Requirements:

  • Liquid Methane/Liquid Oxygen propellant
  • Total propellant mass: ~118,680,000 kg
  • Equivalent to filling about 47,500 Olympic-sized swimming pools with rocket fuel
  • An Olympic-sized swimming pool contains ~2,500,000 liters or 660,430 gallons of water.

Commentary
TL;DR: The rocket equation guarantees that it will be impossible to ever launch enough propellant fuel into space, for getting a life-sustaining human colonization spacecraft from Earth to its destination.

The next section will ask about some things that could potentially happen if O’Neill Cylinders were orbiting the Earth. We obviously don’t want catastrophic scenarios to happen, but we must be willing to consider them, when preparing ourselves for the future. Car accidents happen, train accidents happen, plane accidents happen, and spacecraft accidents have happened.

For the future, the question isn’t if space colonization related accidents will occur. The question is when will they occur, assuming that space colonization is physically and economically feasible in the first place.

3.8. What could cause an O’Neill Cylinder in LEO to fall towards the Earth’s surface?

Let me break down the potential scenarios that could cause an O’Neill Cylinder in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) to descend towards Earth’s surface, exploring this from multiple scientific perspectives.

Primary Descent Mechanisms:

  1. Orbital Decay Due to Atmospheric Drag
    • Even in LEO, extremely thin atmospheric particles create gradual resistance
    • Over time, this constant friction reduces orbital velocity
    • Causes progressive lowering of orbital altitude
    • For a large structure like an O’Neill Cylinder, drag effects would be significantly amplified due to massive surface area
  2. Structural Integrity Failure
    • Micrometeorite impacts
    • Extreme temperature fluctuations causing metal fatigue
    • Radiation-induced material degradation
    • Potential structural breach leading to asymmetric mass distribution
    • Disruption of rotation could cause destabilization
  3. Propulsion/Stabilization System Failures
    • Attitude control thrusters malfunctioning
    • Gyroscopic stabilization systems failing
    • Loss of ability to maintain precise rotational velocity
    • Could induce uncontrolled descent trajectory
  4. Gravitational Perturbations
    • Interactions with Earth’s non-uniform gravitational field
    • Lunar gravitational influences
    • Solar wind and electromagnetic interactions
    • Cumulative effects could gradually alter orbital parameters
  5. Human-Induced Scenarios
    • Deliberate deorbiting
    • Catastrophic system failure
    • Planned end-of-life disposal protocol

To maintain stable LEO, the cylinder would require:

  • Continuous micro-adjustments
  • Advanced materials resistant to space environment
  • Redundant stabilization systems
  • Precise mass distribution
  • Regular maintenance and monitoring

Potential Mitigation Technologies:

  • Ion propulsion systems
  • Electromagnetic orbital maintenance
  • Adaptive structural materials
  • Predictive computational modeling

4. What if humanity builds space travel infrastructure to bring the costs down?

In order for human space travel to be physically possible, space launching infrastructure will have to be built, in order to bypass the limitations of the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation. Without space travel infrastructure and for as long as humanity is bound to using rockets and jet propellants, space travel will be effectively impossible.

Notes about building space travel infrastructure:

  • The book concludes that space elevators, and orbital rings are all probably unfeasible for space launching infrastructure.
  • The book also doesn’t discuss skyhooks/spacehooks, but they may be a viable solution for building infrastructure for both space flight ascent and descent.
    Video: 1,000km Cable to the Stars: The Skyhook - Kurzgesagt.
  • So far, mass drivers have been successful with launching 200kg objects.
  • Space infrastructure would also need to clear space junk from the Earth’s atmosphere, in order to avoid damage to the structures, and increase the safety of future space travel.

5. What could be done to obtain Massive Quantities of Energy?

Leveraging the world’s supply of nuclear energy would grant humanity a lot of energy to work with, but nuclear energy is a non-renewable resource. Many estimates on the Earth’s supply of nuclear energy are also greatly overestimated.

A Dyson swarm isn’t an all or nothing proposal. Its design is fundamentally decentralized. You can start building a Dyson swarm with a single solar energy collector. You can then use the energy collected to do work - work like building another solar energy collector. If the technology for transporting some of the collected energy exists, then the technology for transporting all of it exists (discounting losses of course). And it exists in some form today. It certainly seems plausible that the technology would be vastly easier to do in the vacuum of space and that we can improve it further. Is the premise that it will never be feasible to A. get a first energy collector around the sun and/or B. create energy collectors using materials that can be found off earth?

If a Dyson swarm is not feasible, then it may make more sense to build a bunch of nuclear reactors in space. Humanity could find ways to run nuclear power on Mars and other planetary bodies, and obtain the necessary energy for space colonization that way instead.

6. Asteroid Mining

As mentioned in Futurist Fantasies, it’s physically impossible and impractical to mine materials directly from the asteroid belt. A more plausible and conceivable proposal would thus be to redirect asteroids to crash landing sites where they can be mined, such as Mars or Ceres.

  • We can’t do it yet, and it probably wouldn’t work with larger asteroids.
  • However, it’s still better than mining flying asteroids.
  • This may be a feasible and practical way to saturate planetary colony sites with resources in advance.

Of course though, moving asteroids would require a lot of energy. This method of asteroid mining would also have to be more efficient than mining Mars for resources in order for it to be economically practical.

Currently, mining the Moon for resources and transporting said resources back to Earth seems like that it will be more practical and economical than trying to mine the asteroid belt or colonize Mars.

8. Conclusion On The Practicality Of Space Colonization

In the 1960s, people thought that Humanity would’ve achieved the technological advancements in 2001: A Space Odyssey two decades ago, and that still hasn’t happen by now. People need to recognize that technological process has clearly slowed down, and we’ve nearly reached its limits.

I understand that space colonization is a fascinating topic and would be one of the ultimate achievements that humanity could ever do as a species. I would like to accomplish that goal if it’s possible. But at the end of the day, we also have to recognize that we may fail to ever so. We live in reality, not a sci-fi fantasy world where humans are invincible. There are limits to what humans can do in reality. Sabbine Hossenfelder has reached similar conclusions about Mars colonization.

I’ve shown the calculations for why I believe it’s far too expensive to launch human colonies onto Mars in the near future, especially when it’s still too expensive to be worth colonizing Antarctica or the Sahara Desert. I also didn’t cover over all the physical challenges that would make it virtually impossible (at least with current technology). My judgment is that space colonization won’t be possible for 150-200 years, possibly longer, and probably never (in spite of how much I’d want it to happen). This conclusion is probably contrary to most LessWrongers, but I haven’t seen many posts that satisfactorily answer the physical, logistical, and economical challenges to colonizing space.

Even if humans could live on Mars, it’s worth asking why anyone want to live on Mars when they can live on Earth instead? Even Antarctica is a thousand times better for human life than Mars. I personally don’t understand why people are fantasizing about colonizing Mars when humans haven’t even colonized Antarctica yet.

9. How Humanity Should Prioritize Its Goals

It’s widely known that smaller goals are easier to accomplish than bigger goals, and realistic goals are more practical than less realistic goals. If humanity is serious about space colonization, I believe that we should take baby steps before we colonize Mars and beyond:

  • Finish colonizing the Earth: Colonize Antarctica. Irrigate the Sahara Desert. Et Cetera.
  • Set up a global government for resolving international conflicts, resource scarcity, and overpopulation.
  • Build the most cost-effective space launching infrastructure that is likely to succeed.
  • Colonize the Moon. Set up space launching infrastructure for the Moon.
  • Colonize Mars. Set up space launching infrastructure for Mars.
  • Explore and colonize the rest of space, if possible.
