2. The Constitutional Principles Of The Government

How to govern society is one of the oldest philosophical problems, and we don’t have any ideal solution, just some pragmatic and heuristic ones.

2.1. The Practicality of Constitutions

The de facto power of the United States Constitution is weaker than the de jure power of the Constitution, but this isn’t really surprising. At the end of the day, a Constitution is just a piece of paper created by the government to limit what the government can do. So if the government had the power to create the constitution and enforce it, then it can just as easily have the power to stop enforcing parts of it or even ignore it completely.

But if government is going to exist, it definitely wouldn’t be unreasonable to have a document of rules describing what the government must aim to do, as well as what it can and cannot do. Even most anarchists have implicit rules for their societies, and they would have to have a document that explicitly states the rules of their “societies” if they were actually serious about their ideas. That said, it’s still worth creating a Constitution that states the explicit rules of society and figuring out the best characteristics that a Constitution should have.

Conclusion: In order for a Constitution to have the true power and enforcement that its authors intended it to have, Constitutions must be enforced directly by the people instead of the government. It cannot be stressed enough that the citizens much be vigilant to veto, rebel, and reform if the legal system ever proposes an anti-constitutional update to the legal code, lest a deep state will form. However, I’m not entirely sure how such a society could be structured to make this more likely or even guaranteed to happen, if it’s even possible.

2.2. Political Philosophy And The Goals Of Government

The first and foremost goal of government is to resolve Prisoner’s Dilemmas, Tragedies of the Commons, and Free-Rider Problems. Government comes from the necessity to create a reasonable set of laws that would create a societal framework from which selfish individuals can cooperate together for greater mutual gain (than if they didn’t cooperate together). Cooperation is the foundation of civilization, and the best legal code is the one that enables that to the greatest extent possible.

Instead of calling the relationship between a citizen and the government a “social contract”, it shall instead be called an Implicit Mutual Social Agreement (IMSA). Every time the word “contract” is used, Libertarians and Ancaps complain that it’s not really a contract because they never signed it. And while they’re right about that, they’re missing the point. The idea is that if you want the benefits that society gives you, then you have to comply with society’s rules, even if you don’t like all the rules. You can’t have your cake and eat it too. If you’re going to break the IMSA, then society is justified to prosecute you.

When people say “Never trust the government” or ask “Do you trust the government?”, what do either of those even mean? Government is very complex, there’s local, state, and federal levels, different branches of government, different agencies and departments of government, and many different people who all work for the government.

When conservatives say “Never trust the government”, they will still usually trust government entities like the FDA, the USPS, local utilities, other government agencies, and probably many favored politicians. And when anarchists or libertarians say the tagline, they typically have even less trust and approval for the government as a whole (as it currently exists) compared to conservatives, but even they will probably still count on at least a few functions of government to do things that they depend on.

So what specifically about the government should not be trusted? Any particular departments, agencies, or people filling administrative roles? And generally speaking, what aspects of government can be trusted?

Also, how does one define “trust”? Certainly there are different levels of trust, so what levels of trust can be applied to each component of the government? Are there different types of trust, or are they all the same thing?

3.2. Ensuring Accountability To The People

A politician can do what’s best for the constituents in their district, but that won’t necessarily be what’s best for the entire world as a whole. For example, a local lawmaker/governor might criticize a local company for outsourcing labor since that would take jobs away from their constituents (particularly in the short-term), even if that actually might be what’s best for the entire world as a whole. The company wins because it reduces its labor costs, the newly hired laborers overseas win because they acquire higher-paying jobs, and the community that initially lost jobs eventually will win in the long-term too (assuming that laid-off workers can find new jobs) since they can purchase cheaper goods and services. Conceptually, this concept can be expanded to other assets too, like determining who gets the most government funding and infrastructure, who gets the new Fortune 500 facility, etc. This is one reason why government often works against the people, and unfortunately it happens very often. A more efficient government/reason-system ought to not do this.

  1. Something must be done.
  2. This is something.

The underlying assumption that doing something is always better than doing nothing, regardless of certainty or cost.

7.1. Why Corporal Punishment And Humiliation Are More Practical Punishments Than Making Prisoners Spend Years In Prison

It doesn’t make any sense to punish taxpayers by forcing them to pay money that is used to imprison people who broke the law. Why should a person who is lazy and committed crimes have their existence funded by good laboring people who are actually contributing to society? A better method of punish the lawbreakers without wasting taxpayers’ money.

Instead, I propose that corporal punishment should be used instead of prison sentences. This way, lawbreakers are punished, and taxpayers don’t have to pay the price for keeping the lawbreakers locked up in prison. Physical punishment works in Singapore, and Singapore has some of the lowest per capita crime rates in the world (although race and economic factors undoubtedly help with this too). The people who get punished may be unable to work for a few days while they heal from the injury and are experiencing the pain, but then they have to go back to work, and taxpayers won’t have to subsidize their existence.

Western opposition to corporal punishment is just a cultural thing. In Singapore, there is at least a sizable amount of public support for corporal punishment. And after all, aren’t some crimes deserving of corporal punishment, like rape, massive pyramid schemes, torturing, or severe physical abuse? And if corporal punishment does help deter crime, how could the extent to which it does be proven? Couldn’t Singapore’s lower crime rates also be attributed to its strong economic conditions as well?

People cannot imagine what it’s like to spend 5 years in prison. Especially if it’s often far longer than that, it completely ruins someone’s life. People cannot intuitively understand how bad that is. And once the prisoners finally leave the prison, how are they supposed to get back into the real world and return to a normal lifestyle? They could go back to the outside world, but it would be completely alien to them because everybody would be changed and different. Imprisoning someone for several years is a detriment to everybody in society, including hardworking taxpayers who have to pay for the imprisonment. Some wouldn’t even want to go back out into the real world because they’ve become so familiar with the prison world, hence why people often get re-incarcerated immediately after they leave prison.

Another big factor that could change or possibly improve the effects of physical punishment could be to have it be done publicly. Being caned in the townsquare for everybody to see the person deserving the punishment would be a way to humiliate them, so instead of being a purely psychological force for discouraging/preventing crime, it could also be turned into a social force, possibly even a eugenic force if we consider the following scenario. If someone is seen by the public being punished, then fewer people would work with them, and thus they would make less money, and thus they would have fewer children.

Physical punishment also invokes primal instincts, which could very strongly increase its effectiveness and people’s willingness to avoid it as much as possible. If the brain can be roughly divided into about three parts, then the fear of corporal punishment and strong desire to avoid it works off the most basic structures within the human brain. Both of these proposed punishments are based on incentives, and utilize both psychological and economic principles. Being soft on crime causes crime. And making people spend several years in prison when it’s a very costly and ineffective punishment counts as being soft on crime.

Or alternatively, a prisoner could be offered assisted suicide. Then the taxpayers don’t have to subsidize their existence, and the prisoner then has no further opportunities to pass their genes onto the next generation. In most cases, it would just be better for everybody if people serving multiple life sentences in prison were just sentenced to death instead, but the main reason why this doesn’t happen is due to people’s moral dogmas.

12. Enforcing EPC: Why And How

In order to prevent war, disease, and famine, there must be a maximum population limit enforced for the country’s total number of citizens. In order to enforce that population limit, anybody who wants to have children must acquire a Reproduction License for each child that they want to have. Everybody else who is unauthorized to have (more) children must have mandatory contraception, since that is the only way to ensure that people do not create legally unauthorized offspring, and thus the only way to avoid the truly devastating consequences of overpopulation.

12.1. The Requirements For Being Granted A Reproduction License

  • The parent must be law-abiding and have no criminal record.
  • The parent must have a high school education [at least] and be at least 18 years old.
  • The parents must sign a contractual legal agreement with the government pledging responsibility for raising the child by themselves, and both parents must meet all of these requirements.
  • One of the parents in the child-bearing agreement must be employed in a full-time job.
  • The parent must have paid all their taxes (if any).
  • The parent must pay the Reproduction Tax for having a child, which is set according to market rates with respect to the Supply, Demand, and Price of Reproduction Licenses.

17.3. Addressing Objections To Having A Global Government

Many people have objections against global government that need to addressed:

17.3.1. Why should there be a global government? Can’t nation-state governments suffice?

Because global government is necessary in order to resolve critical game theoretical problems on a global scale. Solving game-theoretic problems is exactly what a government is supposed to do, so a global government simply does that a global scale. Some examples of game-theoretic problems that are best resolved by global government are:

  • overpopulation
  • global warming and carbon emissions
  • enforcing Georgism and the conservation of the Earth’s natural resources
  • regulating the pollution done by each country
  • preventing wars to the fullest extent possible (wars could potentially destroy modern civilization)
  • resolving geopolitical conflicts in order to ensure geopolitical stability
  • Et Cetera

Also, if we don’t build a global government, a de facto global government will take form instead. Today, that is currently the United Nations, combined with the influence of the Chinese and American superpowers. So we might as well build a global government that is less likely to be a dumpster fire like the UN.

17.3.2. But aren’t smaller-scale governments more responsive and representative?

It depends on the situation. But even if this question is true, it doesn’t refute the fact that a global government is still necessary in order to resolve critical game theoretical problems on a global scale, lest you and everybody else on Earth face potentially catastrophic consequences. Even if you are insistent that there should be no global government, a de facto government will arise instead. The current de facto global government consists of the UN, the United States’s power and influence, China’s power and influence, and other international organizations. It would be better to burn the UN to the ground and organize a new global government that would be more apt at regulating and resolving conflicts between the world’s nation-states.

Also, moving problems somewhere else doesn’t accomplish anything. If nation-state governments would be less effective for solving a game-theoretic problem instead of a global government, then a global government should take care of the problem instead.

17.3.3. Wouldn’t a global government be extremely authoritarian?

Maybe, but the same could be said about literally any scale of government. Local governments can be extremely authoritarian, state governments can be extremely authoritarian, national governments can be extremely authoritarian, etc. But the key thing to notice here is that even though it’s obvious that bigger governments have the power to be authoritarian on a greater scale than smaller governments, that hasn’t refuted why it has made sense to build bigger levels of government throughout the history of Humanity, otherwise Nation-States never would’ve formed, nor would the United Nations have been formed less than a century ago. So although a global government might have the potential to be more authoritarian than any other type of government that has previously been known, this is not a good argument because it could also be applied against any other type of government as well before those larger scale forms of government were first created.
