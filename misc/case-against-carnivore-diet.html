2.2. Regarding Weight Loss

Eating a low-carb diet can help people lose weight. Carbs are a basic nutrient your body turns into glucose, or blood sugar, to make energy for your body to work. A low-carb diet, like keto and the early phase of the Atkins Diet, triggers your body into nutritional ketosis. Your liver starts to make ketones – a fuel that kicks in when your body doesn’t have enough sugar to run on – by breaking down fat. To bring about nutritional ketosis, extreme low-carb diets cap your carb intake at less than 10% of your total macronutrient (carbs, fat, and protein) intake. That translates to 20 to 50 grams a day of carbs. Low-carb diets generally shoot for under 26% of nutrition intake, or 130 grams.

Low-carb diets also tend to be much higher in protein than low fat diets. Protein can reduce appetite, boost metabolism and help people hold on to muscle mass despite restricting calories.

The general key to losing weight is to burn more calories than one takes in. It’s that simple. Theoretically, any diet could encourage weight loss as long as caloric intake stays low while the subject gets plenty of exercise. But there are reasons why a carnivore diet may be more effective for weight loss. The high-protein content of the diet may be responsible for most of the carnivore diet’s health effects. Even then, plant proteins are more preferable to animal proteins for reducing the risk of cancer.

2.3. Why Fiber Is Necessary For Human Diets

The idea that fiber isn’t necessary for healthy diets is a misunderstanding of the fact that “Humans can’t gain nutrients from fiber”.

Carnivores don’t eat plants, so they don’t get fiber.

Correct. Instead, carnivores eat bones, feathers, and fur along with the meat they consume, which basically serves the exact same function as fiber. Now obviously, humans should not be eating bones (specifically cooked bones), feathers, or fur, so humans still need a way to get the benefits of fiber if they try the carnivore diet.

If your goal is to just live, then yes, humans don’t need fiber. However, avoiding fiber in the long-term will negatively affect anyone’s health and lead to disease. Low fiber can make you constipated and increase chances of certain cancers, heart disease, diabetes, etc.

If your goal is to live well and long, then fiber is essential, as it provides healthy digestion and healthy gut microbiota, which is essential to overall good health. Those good gut bacteria produce valuable post-biotics that can help with regulating immune function, fixing leaky gut, and fighting chronic inflammation. Fiber also slows digestion, which helps with satiety. This can help people eat less and in turn keep a healthy body weight, which will improve overall health.

Fiber is an antinutrient because it prevents people from absorbing nutrients.

It’s true that the body does not absorb nutrients from fiber directly, but it is critical for your microbiome. The best predictor of a healthy microbiome is to have a diet with a highly diverse set of plants because it provides a diverse set of fiber for our gut. Our microbiome is critical because it regulates our immune system, levels of inflammation, anxiety, and other critical attributes of health.

Low fiber is recommended to help cancer patients to relax their gut and have it do less work. If fiber is recommended against in a harm-state scenario, then that implies that it’s a bad thing.

There are no logical requirements that necessitate that conclusion from the premises. There are also some foods that are not recommended for pregnant women, people with epilepsy, etc that can be beneficial in other scenarios. So, it doesn’t imply anything when some foods are recommended against in strained or dire bodily states.

2.4. Digestive Systems

Is the human digestive tract more similar to a carnivore or a herbivore?
(assume omnivore is not an option)

The human digestive tract is more similar to a carnivore than a herbivore:

  • Stomach acidity: Humans have very acidic stomachs (pH 1.5-2.0), similar to carnivores, which helps break down proteins and kill bacteria in meat. Herbivores typically have much less acidic stomachs.
  • Intestinal length: While humans have longer intestines than typical carnivores, our small intestine is proportionally much shorter than herbivores. Herbivores need extremely long digestive tracts (often 10-12 times their body length) to break down cellulose and extract nutrients from plant matter. Human intestines are about 4-5 times body length.
  • Cecum and appendix: Herbivores have large, functional cecums where bacteria ferment plant material. Humans have a small, largely vestigial appendix and tiny cecum.
  • Enzyme production: Humans produce strong protein-digesting enzymes (pepsin, various proteases) but lack cellulase to break down plant cell walls, unlike many herbivores.
  • Jaw structure: Human jaws move primarily up and down for tearing and crushing, more like carnivores, rather than the side-to-side grinding motion of herbivores.
  • Teeth: While humans have some grinding molars, we also have prominent canines and incisors designed for cutting, unlike the flat grinding teeth that dominate herbivore mouths.

However, humans do show some adaptations for plant consumption, which reflects our evolutionary history as omnivores who could survive on varied diets depending on availability.

A lot of this boils down to how humans evolved to cook their food. Due to this adaptation, humans to avoid eating the kinds of foods that are typically eaten by other animals with huge digestive systems. The digestive systems of other great apes are also more similar to carnivorous systems as well. Yet, we observe that they usually eat fruit and plants most of the time.

A major reason why the human digestive system is different from herbivore digestive systems is that herbivores tend to eat foods that aren’t fit for human consumption. There are still many plants, fruits, and vegetables that are fit for human consumption. Even if the human digestive tract is more similar to a carnivore digestive system, it doesn’t follow that it’s unhealthy for humans to eat foods that are edible for humans.

In any case, there’s no reason to insist that humans and great apes can only be one or the other. They are all omnivores. All life on Earth ultimately follows an opportunistic diet, though some foods will be healthier or less for different animals.

Which would be healthier for humans, a carnivore diet or a herbivore diet?
(assume there are no other options)

A herbivore (plant-based) diet would likely be healthier for humans in the long term, despite our digestive anatomy being more similar to carnivores.

  • Cardiovascular health: Extensive research shows plant-based diets are associated with lower rates of heart disease, lower blood pressure, and better cholesterol profiles.
  • Saturated fat and cholesterol from animal products are linked to increased cardiovascular risk.
  • Cancer prevention: Large epidemiological studies indicate lower rates of colorectal, prostate, and other cancers among populations eating primarily plant-based diets.
  • Processed and red meats are classified as carcinogens by the WHO.
  • Longevity: The world’s longest-lived populations (Blue Zones) tend to eat predominantly plant-based diets with minimal animal products.
  • Diabetes and metabolic health: Plant-based diets are associated with better insulin sensitivity and lower rates of type 2 diabetes.
  • Potential challenges with carnivore diets: A pure carnivore diet risks deficiencies in vitamin C, fiber, and various phytonutrients. It may also lead to kidney stress from high protein loads and increased risk of certain chronic diseases.

What’s wrong with the pro-carnivore-diet claim that humans don’t need to eat carbohydrates since their bodies can do gluconeogenesis instead?

The pro-carnivore diet claim about gluconeogenesis overlooks several important physiological realities:

  1. Metabolic inefficiency: While your body can indeed create glucose from non-carbohydrate sources through gluconeogenesis, this process is metabolically costly and less efficient than directly consuming carbohydrates. It requires additional energy expenditure to convert proteins and fats into glucose.
  2. Protein requirements: Relying exclusively on gluconeogenesis means your body must break down either dietary protein or your own muscle tissue to create glucose. This increases overall protein requirements beyond what’s needed for normal tissue maintenance and growth.
  3. Brain function: Although the brain can partially adapt to using ketones for energy during carbohydrate restriction, it still requires some glucose. The brain consumes roughly 120g of glucose daily in a typical adult, placing significant demands on gluconeogenesis in a zero-carb diet.
  4. Physiological stress: Chronic gluconeogenesis can increase cortisol levels and potentially create hormonal imbalances due to the metabolic stress of constant glucose production.
  5. Evolutionary context: While our ancestors could survive periods without carbohydrates, most traditional hunter-gatherer societies consumed mixed diets including plant foods when available, suggesting our biology evolved with at least some carbohydrate intake.
  6. Individual variation: Some people adapt better to very low carbohydrate diets than others due to genetic factors and metabolic differences.

While gluconeogenesis is a remarkable adaptive mechanism that allows humans to survive without dietary carbohydrates, relying on it exclusively as a long-term strategy may not be optimal for most people’s health and metabolic function.

If everybody ate as much meat as what carnivore proponents recommend, the world life expectancy would plummet dramatically. This is really not up for debate. It’s a fact, even if some people don’t like it and cannot accept the truth.

For most people, I would endorse the advice made by Viva Longevity and Walter Willett. However, I also recognize that the healthiest diets depend on genetics to some extent (e.g. the Inuit are some of the only people in the world who are well-adapted to a completely carnivorous diet). A diet that works for one person won’t necessarily work for another person, so individual experimentation is necessary for anyone to figure out what diet is best for them. Nothing in this essay should be considered as medical, health, or dieting advice.
