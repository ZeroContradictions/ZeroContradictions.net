3. What is overpopulation?

Overpopulation is when there is an excessive number of occupants (people, animals, plants, etc.) in a particular area; specifically, when the number of occupants exceeds the ability of that area to provide for them. Overpopulation is preceded by ecological overshoot.

The primary misconception of overpopulation is that it only depends on the number of organisms/people. This view assumes that the only factor that matters for overpopulation is the number of organisms within the environment. It further assumes that, if a certain number of individuals can be supported in the present, there’ll be no problems in the future, which I’ve debunked here.

This view is wrong and very ignorant about biology. Overpopulation is determined by at least four parameters:

  • Number of creatures (usually of a single species) in an area
  • An area and its resources
  • The average ecological footprint per creature (for humans, this is the average standard of living per capita)
  • A timeframe
  • (for humans) The technology and substitution options available to those creatures
  • (for humans) The distribution of wealth and opportunities (which is heavily influenced by the system of property norms).

4. Hasn’t Malthusianism been debunked?

Thomas Malthus is widely regarded as the most famous early intellectual of population dynamics. In 1798, Malthus wrote “An Essay on the Principles of Population”, stating:

“Must it not then be acknowledged by an attentive examiner of the histories of mankind, that in every age and in every state in which man has existed, or does now exist:

That the increase of population is necessarily limited by the means of subsistence.

That population does invariably increase when the means of subsistence increase.

And that the superior power of population it repressed, and the actual population kept equal to the means of subsistence, by misery and vice?“

Malthus was correct that human populations had historically been limited by subsistence farming (at the time he wrote the essay), but he was wrong that this would always be the case in the future because he didn’t correctly anticipate the discovery of additional energy sources, and didn’t see the contributions people like Norman Borlaug made through genetically engineering crops. So Malthus’ thesis about linear production versus exponential consumption rates was incorrect. However, what Malthus’s claim in the essay is different from the definition of Malthusianism.

Malthusianism: The theory that potentially exponential population growth can surpass the linear growth of an environment’s carrying capacity (including its food supply and other resources), which eventually causes members of the population to die off until settling back down to the carrying capacity.

Neo-Malthusianism: The theory and concern that overpopulation (and overconsumption) may increase resource depletion and/or environmental degradation and lead to ecological collapse or other hazards. Neo-Malthusianism is often coupled with advocacy of human population planning to ensure sufficient resources for current and future human populations as well as for other species.

We are not defending what Malthus wrote in 1798 (that populations are limited by subsistence farming). Instead, we are arguing in favor of Neo-Malthusianism, which is a completely different concept that can be defended by an upgraded knowledge of history, population dynamics, and evolutionary reasoning. It does not follow that Malthus being wrong means that the concepts of overpopulation and ecological overshoot are incorrect, not applicable to humans, or not a risk which bears investigation. We support well-grounded Neo-Malthusian solutions as a solution to the overpopulation disaster that is bound to plague humanity within the 21st century.

22. How will the government determine the carrying capacity and the Legal Maximum Population Limit (LMPL) of the country?

LMPL: Legal Maximum Population Limit

For starters, we should consider the definition and causes of overpopulation, which we shall restate here for convenience. Overpopulation is determined by at least four parameters:

  • Number of creatures (usually of a single species) in an area
  • An area and its resources
  • The average ecological footprint per creature (for humans, this is the average standard of living per capita)
  • A timeframe
  • (for humans) The technology and substitution options available to those creatures
  • (for humans) The distribution of wealth and opportunities (which is heavily influenced by the system of property norms).

If we want to estimate what the carrying capacity of the Earth is, some reasonable calculations to start with would be to multiply the number of people living in the society by the expected consumption of various resources that we would expect of each person for food, water, electricity, living space, etc. These calculations could be compared to what we expect the supplies for each of these resources to be, and the resource with the lowest supply would determine what number of people the LMPL should be allocated for the foreseeable future.

The hardest factor to consider for overpopulation would probably be the timeframe since it is very difficult to predict the future, but we predict that it will be easier to take the timeframe into consideration if the population control system is implemented, the population stabilizes, and humanity learns from its mistakes.

However, we will note that since timeframe and changing circumstances are the most unpredictable factor for affecting an environment’s carrying capacity, that drastically reduces the certainty of any predictions that could be made.

22.1. Calculating multiple different carrying capacities for differing conditions

Although modern first-world civilizations have the highest carrying capacity of any other time in history, we know that that carrying capacity of that environment is currently declining due to our exhaustion of the Earth’s scarce resources. We can also predict that if any node of the interconnected systems that enable us to live our current lifestyles were to fail (destruction of critical infrastructure, labor strikes, closure of trade routes, the depletion of fossil fuel reserves, warfare, etc), that would jeopardize our lifestyles and cause the carrying capacity to fall.

That being said, it makes sense to calculate multiple different theoretical carrying capacities for each region, given a set of different conditions. For example, we could predict carrying capacities for the following societal structures:

  • Hunter-gatherer society
  • Neolithic, agricultural society
  • Pre-Industrial 17-18th century society
  • Industrial 19-20th century society, with global free trade and globalization
  • Modern, first-world 21st century society
  • Modern 21st century society with Georgism and other economic solutions for raising the carrying capacity to the furthest extent possible

The purpose of calculating how these different conditions would change the carrying capacity of the environment would be to theorize what we should set the new LMPL to be if modern civilization were to collapse, so that we can prepare for a collapse (if it were predictable).

The LMPL would be determined by the carrying capacity whose conditions we think are the most probable for the future.
